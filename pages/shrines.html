Shrines

Japan is an interesting melting pot of religious beliefs, and nothing embodies that better than the drastically differing shrines that can be found all over Tokyo. Below are a few suggestions of popular shrines to visit while in Tokyo.

Meiji Jingu Shrine:

A popular Shinto shrine located 10 minutes from both the Harajuku and Yoyogi train stations, it offers a peaceful oasis escape of tranquility from the busy streets of Tokyo. Nested within a thick forest of trees, this shrine offers all of the normal activities (i.e. making wishes) as well as a treasury that houses valuables once owned by the late Emperor Meiji and Empress Shoken that can only be seen here. In addition, the shrine offers a large inner garden that visitors may see for a small fee and is very popular in June when the flowers bloom. It was rebuilt after its destruction during World War ll. Visit Official Website

Sensoji Temple:

The oldest temple in Japan, Sensoji Temple (also known as Asakusa Kannon Temple) is a Buddhist temple well known for its colorful buildings and annual events (i.e. Sanja Matsuri). A large sprawling market street extents for 200 meters between the first and second gates of the temple, and is a popular place to find many souvenirs and traditional snacks only found in Japan. Visit Official Website

Tsukiji Hongan-ji Temple:

Among the newer temples built in Japan, Tsukiji Hongan-ji Temple stands out for its unique Indian inspired architecture that features splashes of Western inspired features as well. The inside of the temple also supports a blend of cultures by mixing the beautiful Japanese ornamentation with elements from other cultures, such as an organ from Germany. Additionally, this temple doubles as a memorial for the late Hideto Matsumoto, whom was a popular rock star in Japan from the band "X Japan". Visit Official Website
